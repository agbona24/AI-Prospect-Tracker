The best lead generation tool depends on what kind of lead you're chasing. Apollo.io and Hunter.io are strongest for finding contacts at companies that already have a web presence and a LinkedIn footprint. Snov.io is a solid budget all-rounder for email finding and light sequencing. Runvax is built for a narrower, warmer job: finding local businesses that have **no website yet** and turning each one into a ready-to-send email, WhatsApp message, or proposal in one click.

None of these tools are strictly "better" in the abstract — they're built for different targets. Here's how they actually compare.

| Manage a pipeline without a CRM subscription | Runvax (built-in Kanban) |

## How These Tools Actually Differ

The core difference is **data source**. Apollo, Hunter, and Snov.io all pull from static or semi-static databases built by scraping company websites, LinkedIn, and public web data. That's excellent for finding a marketing director at a 200-person SaaS company. It's much weaker for finding the salon on the third street in Ibadan that has a Google Business Profile but no website and no LinkedIn presence at all — because there's nothing to scrape.

Runvax queries live business listing data (Google's business index) in real time, which is where small local businesses actually show up, and flags whether each one has a detectable website. That's a fundamentally different use case from enterprise contact databases, and it's why the two categories don't fully overlap.

## Where Apollo and Hunter Genuinely Win

To be fair to the established players: if your job is finding the CFO's email at a Series B SaaS company, Apollo's database and filtering (by title, seniority, tech stack, funding stage) is more mature than anything a newer tool offers. Hunter's domain search and email verification are fast and reliable for that exact task. Both have years of deliverability tooling, integrations, and enterprise trust behind them. If your leads already have websites and LinkedIn profiles, these are the right tools — full stop.
